I am a postdoctoral researcher at The Broad Institute of MIT and Harvard co-advised by Aviv Regev and Fei Chen.

I am interested in how different cell types in tissues respond to external signals. Current single-cell methods do not measure the heterogeneity in intracellular protein activities that transform signals into downstream gene regulation, posing a major gap in our understanding of how biochemical mechanisms relate to genome-wide changes. To bridge this gap, I develop single cell proteogenomic tools and computational frameworks to identify the drivers of cell state changes using multi-modal information.

I received my Ph. D. in Systems Biology from Harvard University as a Paul and Daisy Soros Fellow advised by Roy Kishony. I studied evolutionary dynamics, in the context of how pathogens evolve antibiotic resistance during respiratory infections. I worked closely with clinical collaborators at Boston Children's Hospital to develop experimental and computational approaches that uncovered how pathogens evolve across space and time in our body, directly from patient samples.

I received my S.B. in Biological Engineering from MIT with a minor in applied international studies.
